## Part I · Cosmic Acceleration as the col(F)/ker(F) Boundary of the Late Universe

### I.1 A Thought Experiment: The Dimmer Candle

A standard candle is an object whose true brightness is known. Place one nearby and one far away, and the far one looks fainter by a precise, calculable amount — fainter in proportion to the square of its distance. Astronomers have such a candle: a particular kind of exploding star, a Type Ia supernova, that detonates always at very nearly the same true brightness. Measure how faint a distant supernova appears, and you have measured how far away it is. Measure its redshift, and you have measured how much the universe has expanded since its light departed. Collect many supernovae across many distances, and you have mapped the expansion history of the universe.

In the late 1990s two teams did exactly this, and the distant supernovae came back *too faint*. They were farther away than a universe slowing under its own gravity could have placed them. The only way to put them where they appeared was for the expansion to have been *speeding up* — for the universe, over the last several billion years, to have been pushed apart at an ever-increasing rate.

Now ask the question that has occupied cosmology ever since. The supernovae tell you, with great precision, *that* the expansion accelerates and *by how much* at each epoch. They give you the expansion history — the scale factor as a function of time, the full record of how fast the universe has grown. But they do not tell you *what is doing the pushing*. The dimmer candle is a measurement of the expansion. It is not a measurement of the cause.

### II.1 A Thought Experiment: Two Machines, One Output

Imagine two sealed machines, side by side, each with a single dial on the front that reads the expansion rate of a model universe. You may turn no knobs; you may only read the dial. Your task: determine what is inside each machine.

Two completely different interiors: one a new ingredient, the other a new law.
